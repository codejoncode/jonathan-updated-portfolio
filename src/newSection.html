Key Legal Questions Identified

Upon receiving the petition, the Respondent independently identified the following dispositive legal questions — without attorney assistance — by reading Indiana statutes and applying them to the documented facts.

Q 01

Are there undisclosed marital assets, and does their omission violate Indiana's one-pot rule?

A 01

Indiana Code § 31-15-7-4 requires all assets of both parties to be disclosed, regardless of whose name they are held in. The Respondent, appearing pro se, independently located this rule, understood it, and disclosed every account — including an $82 forex account, two zero-balance accounts, and a possibly dormant former-employer retirement plan.

The Petitioner, represented by licensed counsel, disclosed only the marital home and vehicle — the two assets she seeks to claim. Her retirement account through a prior employer, accumulated during the marriage; any employer benefits through her current managerial employment; and her interest in a deceased parent's estate are wholly absent from the petition. Pre-filing text communications establish that Petitioner was on written notice that Respondent had identified the retirement accounts as marital assets before the petition was filed.

⚠ Petitioner: Selective Disclosure ✓ Respondent: Full Transparency
Q 02

Does the Petitioner's work schedule support a viable sole-custody claim?

A 02

Indiana Code § 31-17-2-8 directs courts to consider, among other factors, the interaction and interrelationship of the child with each parent, and the mental and physical health of all individuals involved. Courts have consistently emphasized the primacy of the "primary caregiver" factor.

The Petitioner works 50–60+ hours per week as a General Manager, including late nights (past midnight) and early mornings, with emergency call-ins on days off. On the day service was executed, the Petitioner was called in to work and did not return until after midnight; the following night she did not return until after 3:00 a.m. The Respondent was present with all four children on both occasions. The Petitioner's schedule, by her own pattern of conduct, has made the Respondent the de facto primary caregiver for approximately eight years.

⚠ Sole Custody Claim: Unsupported by Schedule ✓ Respondent: Established Caregiver
Q 03

Does the petition propose any childcare plan, and is sole custody viable without one?

A 03

A petition for sole custody implicitly represents that the petitioning parent can provide primary daily care. The Petitioner's petition does not identify who will supervise four children — including a six-year-old in kindergarten — during late-night shifts, overnight absences, or emergency call-ins. No childcare plan, no alternative caregiver, no structured arrangement for morning school-bus supervision or after-school care is proposed.

On the day of service, the Petitioner delegated a six-year-old's school pickup to a fourteen-year-old sibling rather than contacting the Respondent, who was home and available. This is direct evidentiary documentation of the gap that would become permanent if the Respondent were removed from the home. A licensed attorney has a professional obligation to evaluate whether a sole-custody claim is supportable before filing it.

⚠ Fatal Defect: No Childcare Plan
Q 04

What is the documented caregiving history, and over what period does it span?

A 04

Since approximately 2018 — when the Petitioner's promotion brought 50–60+ hour work weeks — the Respondent has served as the children's principal caregiver. This period spans roughly eight years, representing more than half the lives of three of the four children and the entirety of the youngest child's life. Documented responsibilities include: primary morning supervision and school-bus duty; after-school supervision every day; daily meal preparation; academic tutoring (preventing one child from being held back twice); a structured summer educational program of the Respondent's own design; health-routine monitoring; physical engagement and discipline; and exclusive overnight caregiving during a prior period when the Petitioner worked evening and overnight shifts five days per week. The Petitioner's own conduct confirms her trust — she raised no objection to the Respondent taking children on out-of-state overnight trips without her.

✓ Respondent: Eight-Year Documented Caregiver Role
Q 05

Are there contradictions between the petition and the Petitioner's prior written statements?

A 05

The petition requests child support and payment of marital expenses from the Respondent. The Respondent holds a text message from the Petitioner — made before the petition was filed — in which she states she never sought those payments. A petition filed under a party's name and signature must reflect that party's actual instructions. A sworn contradiction between the petition's relief-requested section and the Petitioner's prior written statement raises a direct credibility issue that this Court should resolve before entering any financial orders.

⚠ Petitioner: Credibility Issue — Text vs. Petition
Q 06

Does either party have alternative housing, and how does that affect exclusive possession?

A 06

Indiana Code § 31-15-4-8 authorizes provisional exclusive possession orders. Courts weigh, among other factors, which party has alternative housing options and the hardship an order would impose. The Respondent has no alternative housing — the marital home is the only home the children have ever known, and granting exclusive possession to the Petitioner would render the Respondent immediately homeless. The Petitioner, by contrast, has on multiple occasions spent entire nights at her mother's residence in a neighboring state, demonstrating an established alternative shelter option. Awarding exclusive possession to a party with demonstrated access to alternative accommodations over a party who would be rendered homeless is a factor courts may weigh toward denying exclusive possession at the provisional stage.

✓ Respondent: No Alternative Housing — Favors Denial of Exclusive Possession Order
Q 07

Does involuntary unemployment disqualify a parent from primary custody or bar child support deferral?

A 07

Indiana Code § 31-17-2-8 does not list employment status as a disqualifying custody factor. Courts focus on stability, structured routines, and the quality of the parent-child relationship. Indiana has consistently held that unemployment, when involuntary and combined with active income-seeking efforts, does not defeat a custody claim and may actually reflect greater daily availability to the children. The Respondent's layoff was involuntary; he has since obtained a professional license specifically to generate income and has applied for hundreds of positions, all documented. Ordering child support from an involuntarily unemployed parent who is actively seeking work would impair his ability to pursue employment — a result inconsistent with the children's long-term best interest.

→ Involuntary Unemployment: Not Disqualifying Under Indiana Law
Q 08

Are there life insurance policies, and does the Petitioner have the ability to alter or cancel them?

A 08

The Respondent identified a specific risk: if the Petitioner holds a life insurance policy on the Respondent's life — as spouses commonly do — she could cancel it, change the beneficiary, or borrow against it during the pendency of the proceedings. Indiana provisional order statutes authorize preservation-of-assets orders. The Respondent requested that the Court order disclosure of any such policies and prohibit alteration of beneficiary designations pending final hearing, ensuring any death benefit would inure to the minor children rather than to the Petitioner. This is a protective rather than offensive request — the Respondent holds no comparable policy and sought no reciprocal benefit for himself.

→ Protective Relief Requested — Children's Interest

Statutory Framework

Ind. Code § 31-15-7-4
The One-Pot Rule — Marital Asset Disclosure
Directs courts to divide the marital estate, which consists of all assets of either party, regardless of when acquired, how titled, or whose name they are held in. Both parties are obligated to disclose all assets to the Court.
Applied here: Petitioner omitted retirement accounts, estate interest, and personal financial accounts. Respondent disclosed all accounts including those with zero or nominal value.
Ind. Code § 31-15-7-5
Just and Reasonable Division — Unequal Division Authority
Provides that the presumption of equal division may be rebutted. Courts may award an unequal division where one party has contributed disproportionately to the marital estate, where assets have been dissipated or concealed, or where economic justice requires deviation from equal shares.
Applied here: Years of uncompensated mortgage payments on property held in spouse's name; proceeds of sale never received; capital improvements funded by Respondent.
Ind. Code § 31-17-2-8
Best Interests of the Child — Custody Factors
Lists factors for custody determination including: age and sex of the child; wishes of the child; interaction with each parent; child's adjustment to home, school, and community; mental and physical health of all parties; evidence of domestic violence; and the ability of each party to foster a positive relationship between the children and the other parent.
Applied here: Respondent is the documented primary caregiver; Petitioner's schedule leaves children without parental supervision; Petitioner has made threats that she would deny the Respondent access to the children.
Ind. Code § 31-15-4-8
Provisional Orders — Scope and Limitations
Authorizes courts to enter provisional orders during pendency of dissolution proceedings covering custody, possession of the marital home, support, and use of marital property. Provisional orders are temporary and do not determine final disposition of marital property.
Applied here: Exclusive possession of the home at provisional stage does not constitute a final property award; Court should defer final disposition until complete financial disclosures are received.
Indiana Trial Rule 6(B)
Enlargement of Time — Automatic Extension
Provides for an automatic one-time enlargement of time to respond to a petition when notice is filed on or before the original response deadline. The extension is not discretionary when properly invoked.
Applied here: Respondent independently located this rule, invoked it on time, and extended his response deadline by 30 days — allowing adequate time to conduct research and draft responsive documents.
Ind. Code § 31-17-2-8(7)
Domestic Violence & Coercive Control — Custody Factor
Directs courts to consider evidence of domestic or family violence in the custody determination. Courts have extended this to include coercive control behaviors, including threats to deny a parent access to children as leverage.
Applied here: Petitioner threatened on at least two occasions that Respondent would "never see" the children if he pursued separation — documented coercive control raising direct relevance to best-interests analysis.

Case Law Research

Marital Assets
Aurey v. Aurey
Indiana Court of Appeals — Marital Estate / One-Pot Doctrine
Indiana's one-pot rule includes all assets of either spouse regardless of when acquired, how titled, or whether separately held. Inherited assets that entered the marital estate are subject to division. Courts have broad discretion to make unequal awards where one party has concealed assets or received a disproportionate benefit during the marriage.
Applied to Petitioner's omitted retirement accounts and estate interest. Even if originally separate property, assets used during the marriage or improved by marital contributions enter the marital pot.
Custody
Custody — Primary Caregiver Doctrine
Indiana Appellate Courts — Ind. Code § 31-17-2-8 Application
Indiana courts consistently give substantial weight to the parent who has served as the primary day-to-day caregiver. Employment status alone is not a disqualifying factor; courts focus on the quality and continuity of the parent-child relationship, stability of the home environment, and structured routines. A parent who is home full-time and providing consistent care is not disfavored relative to an employed parent whose schedule limits daily availability.
Respondent's eight-year documented caregiving role, including exclusive overnight caregiving during prior years, directly satisfies this standard. Petitioner's 50–60 hour weekly schedule and documented overnight absences undermine a sole-custody claim.
Disclosure
Asset Concealment — Equitable Remedy
Indiana Appellate Courts — Dissipation / Concealment Doctrine
Where a party conceals assets or fails to make complete disclosure, Indiana courts have authority to: (1) draw an adverse inference against the concealing party; (2) charge the concealed or dissipated asset against that party's share of the marital estate; and (3) award the other spouse a greater portion of the disclosed marital estate as an equitable offset. Deliberate concealment by represented counsel is treated with heightened skepticism.
Petitioner's failure to disclose retirement accounts, despite being on written notice from Respondent that those accounts were identified as marital assets, is directly subject to this doctrine.
Provisional
Exclusive Possession — Hardship Balancing
Indiana Courts — Provisional Orders Practice
Courts considering exclusive possession at the provisional stage weigh comparative hardship. Granting exclusive possession to a party who would not be rendered homeless, at the expense of a party who has no alternative housing, is a factor courts may weigh toward denial or modification. Provisional possession orders are temporary and do not resolve property rights.
Petitioner has demonstrated access to alternative shelter; Respondent has none. Exclusive possession would render the Respondent homeless and separate him from the children he has been caring for daily.
Credibility
Petition Inconsistency — Credibility Inference
Indiana Courts — Trial Practice / Credibility
Where a party's sworn or filed representations contradict their prior written communications, courts are entitled to draw negative credibility inferences. A petition that requests relief the petitioner privately disclaimed in contemporaneous text messages raises questions about whether the petition accurately reflects the party's actual wishes and instructions — and whether its representations should be taken at face value.
Petitioner's text message stating she "never asked for" child support and marital expense payments directly contradicts the filed petition. Court should require sworn clarification before entering financial orders.
Best Interests
Parental Relationship Facilitation
Ind. Code § 31-17-2-8(9) — Court Application
A parent's willingness to facilitate and support the children's relationship with the other parent is a statutory custody factor. Documented threats to deny a parent access to children — used as leverage or retaliation — reflect directly on the threatening parent's fitness to hold primary custody and their likely future conduct if awarded it.
Petitioner threatened on at least two occasions that Respondent would "never see" the children if he pursued separation. This is dispositive on the relationship-facilitation factor.
